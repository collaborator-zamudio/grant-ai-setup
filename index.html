Broad overview, AI vs. human judgment, and use cases with examples

⚖️
When to use AI vs. when to do it yourself Judgment call ▾
  • 1
    Good fit for AI: repetitive organizing (sorting files, renaming, tagging contacts), first drafts of emails or documents, summarizing long threads or PDFs, research that would take you an hour of searching.
  • 2
    Keep it human: anything where a wrong answer is costly and hard to verify — financial decisions, anything sent externally without review, decisions about people (hiring, sensitive conversations).
  • 3
    The honest middle ground: AI drafts, you approve. This is the default mode for Cowork — it proposes a plan, shows its work, and waits for confirmation on anything that deletes or sends something. Treat the first output as a draft, not a final answer, until you've used a given workflow enough times to trust it.
📋
Use cases with real examples Worked examples ▾
Example 1 — Contact cleanup (Claude Cowork). You ask Claude to scan a folder containing your exported iCloud contacts CSV and flag duplicates. Claude reads the file, identifies near-duplicate entries (same person, slightly different spelling or missing fields), and produces a cleaned version plus a log of what it merged — without you opening the spreadsheet once.
Example 2 — Inbox triage (any of the three). You ask the agent to read your last 20 unread emails and summarize anything that needs a reply this week, grouped by urgency. You stay in control of what actually gets sent — the agent's job is surfacing what matters, not sending replies unsupervised.
Example 3 — Research and synthesis (Claude or Gemini). You ask for a comparison of two service providers, with pricing and pros/cons pulled from their websites, delivered as a short table. This is the same kind of task this crash course itself was built from.

Notice the pattern across all three: you give a clear goal and a clear boundary (what file, what folder, what it shouldn't touch), and the agent does the legwork. The next section shows exactly how to phrase that.

Part 3

Implementation — setup, prompting, and structure

What to install, how to structure inputs, and the framework for prompting

⚙️
Step 1 — Install and activate Claude Cowork Start here ▾
  • 1
    Go to claude.com/download, download Claude Desktop for Mac, install it, and sign in with the Pro account.
  • 2
    In the app, find the mode selector at the top — Chat, Cowork, and Code. Click Cowork.
  • 3
    On first run, Cowork downloads a small virtual machine (~2GB) that it works inside of — this is a safety feature, isolating what it does from the rest of the Mac. Let it finish before continuing.
  • 4
    When prompted to select a folder, do not point it at the whole Documents folder or home directory. Create a new, dedicated folder first — covered next.
📁
Step 2 — Build the folder structure Most important step ▾
This is the single biggest lever for getting good results. Cowork performs poorly when it starts from zero every session. The fix is giving it a small set of files it reads automatically — your context, your rules, your way of working — before it touches anything.

Recommended structure — create this on the Desktop or in Documents:

Claude-Work/ ├── context/ │ └── about-me.md (who you are, how you write, what to avoid) ├── system/ │ └── rules.md (things Claude should never do without asking) ├── projects/ │ ├── contacts-cleanup/ │ ├── email-triage/ │ └── notion-sync/ └── outputs/ (where finished work lands)
  • 1
    about-me.md: a short file (aim for under 2,000 words) describing who Grant is, his role, his tone preferences, and anything Claude should know before starting any task. Keep it tight — a long, rambling file actually hurts performance because Claude has to read through more noise.
  • 2
    rules.md: the boundaries. Example: "Never delete a file without asking first." "Always ask before sending an email." "If something is ambiguous, ask rather than guess."
  • 3
    projects/: one subfolder per ongoing task. This keeps each project's files contained and lets Claude focus only on what's relevant to that task.
  • 4
    outputs/: tell Claude explicitly to save finished work here. This makes it easy to find what it produced without digging through the project folders.
🌐
Step 3 — Set Global Instructions 5 minutes ▾

Global Instructions tell Claude how to behave in every Cowork session, regardless of which folder it's working in. Set this once in Settings → Cowork → Global Instructions.

Starting template — paste this in and adjust as needed:

You are my working partner inside this workspace. Start by reading: 1. context/about-me.md 2. system/rules.md 3. the relevant folder inside projects/ Do not execute immediately if the goal, audience, or output format is unclear. Ask questions first when missing context could weaken the result. Do not invent facts, decisions, dates, numbers, or approvals. Make uncertainty visible. Save final outputs in outputs/.

This single paragraph removes most of the vague, generic-feeling responses people complain about with Cowork. It gives Claude a starting behavior before any task-specific context arrives.

✍️
Step 4 — The prompting framework: RTF How to ask ▾
The simplest framework that works almost every time: Role, Task, Format (RTF). You don't need anything more complicated than this for the vast majority of requests.
  • R
    Role — what perspective should it take? ("You are organizing my contacts list" or just skip this for simple tasks — it's optional, not always needed.)
  • T
    Task — the specific, concrete thing you want done. Vague: "clean up my contacts." Specific: "find duplicate contacts in contacts.csv and merge them, keeping the entry with the most complete information."
  • F
    Format — what should the output look like? "Save the cleaned file as contacts-clean.csv in the same folder, and give me a short list of what you merged."

A full example put together:

Find duplicate contacts in projects/contacts-cleanup/contacts.csv. A duplicate is the same person with a slightly different name spelling, missing phone number, or missing email. Merge each duplicate pair, keeping whichever entry has the most complete information. Save the result as contacts-clean.csv in the same folder. Give me a list of which entries you merged so I can spot-check them.

Notice this prompt never says "you are an expert at..." — that's the Role piece, and it's genuinely optional for simple tasks. Add it back in for writing tasks where tone matters: "You are writing in my voice, casual but direct" changes the output meaningfully.

🔒
Step 5 — Permissions and safety defaults Don't skip ▾
  • 1
    When Claude wants to do something irreversible (delete a file, send an email), it always asks first — this is a hard rule in Cowork, not optional behavior.
  • 2
    Only grant folder access to the dedicated Claude-Work folder, not your whole Documents directory or home folder. Copy files in that you want worked on, rather than pointing Claude at originals.
  • 3
    Check in early on longer tasks — glance at the first few steps Claude takes to confirm it understood the goal, rather than walking away and checking back an hour later.
  • 4
    Watch for scope creep. Cowork occasionally does more than asked. If you see it heading somewhere unintended, redirect immediately rather than letting it compound.

Appendix

Hardware comparison — MacBook Air M5 vs. Lenovo

Why the Mac is still the right call, even though the Lenovo wins on paper specs

⚡
MacBook Air M5 vs. Lenovo Yoga Slim 7x (Snapdragon X2 Elite) Resolved ▾
Recommendation: stick with the MacBook Air M5. On raw NPU throughput, Qualcomm's Snapdragon X2 Elite chip is genuinely faster, but that gap doesn't translate into a better experience for Grant's actual workflow — cloud-based agents and Whisper-based dictation — rather than large local model inference.
Category MacBook Air M5 Lenovo Yoga Slim 7x (Snapdragon X2 Elite)
NPU / AI TOPS ~38 TOPS (16-core Neural Engine) 80 TOPS (Hexagon NPU) — wins on raw throughput
AI benchmark score ~57,000 (Geekbench AI) ~88,600 (Geekbench AI) — wins decisively
Max RAM 32GB ceiling Up to 32GB on this model (some Windows machines go to 128GB) — matters only for large local models
Claude Desktop + Cowork Native, mature, day-one support Full feature parity as of Feb 2026, but newer and requires enabling Hyper-V/VM Platform manually
App compatibility No emulation; native macOS apps ARM (Windows on Snapdragon) still needs Prism emulation for non-native x86 apps — 15-30% slower
Battery life Strong, all-day Excellent — up to 25-29 hrs real-world, edges out the Air
Price (comparable config) ~$1,299 (24GB/512GB) ~$1,599-1,699 (32GB/1TB, X2 Elite config)
Ecosystem fit Native iCloud/Contacts.app integration — directly feeds the contacts pipeline Would require extra steps to bridge iCloud data onto Windows
  • 1
    Why the NPU gap doesn't matter here: the 80 vs 38 TOPS difference shows up mainly in local, on-device AI model inference — running large language models directly on the chip without internet. Grant's plan is built around cloud-hosted Claude and Gemini, where the heavy computation happens on Anthropic's and Google's servers, not the laptop's NPU.
  • 2
    Where Windows narrows the gap: Claude Cowork reached full feature parity on Windows in February 2026 — file access, MCP connectors, plugins, and multi-step task execution all work identically to macOS now. This removes what used to be the strongest argument for Mac.
  • 3
    Why Mac still wins for Grant specifically: the iCloud contacts export (Contacts.app → vCard) that feeds the Notion CRM pipeline is Apple-native. Recreating it on Windows would mean keeping an Apple device around just for that step, or relying on iCloud for Windows, which has historically been less reliable for Contacts sync.

If Grant's plans expand later to include large local models (60B+ parameters) running entirely offline, the Snapdragon X2 Elite's higher RAM ceiling and TOPS would become a real advantage. For the current scope — cloud agents, voice input, Notion, contacts — that scenario isn't in play.
